With 90-minute blocks and a four-day week, you have a luxury of time that allows for a "Studio" atmosphere. This is enough time for students to move past "following a tutorial" and into "Information Architecture" and "Systems Design."

Since you are prioritizing **CSS Grid (Template Areas)** and **Media Queries** by the end of the semester, these projects are designed to move from rigid layouts to fluid, area-based designs.

Here are two suggested major projects for the 8-week mark and the Semester Finale.

---

### Project 1: The "Digital Crate" (8-Week Mark)
**Focus:** Visual Hierarchy, Box Model Mastery, and Semantic HTML.
**The Concept:** Students create a high-fidelity documentation site for a specific "collection." This could be a discography of a musician, a catalog of vintage sneakers, or a guide to a specific video game universe.

* **The In-Depth Requirement:** Instead of just a "gallery," students must design **three distinct page types**: 
    1.  A Landing Page (The Hook).
    2.  The Archive (The Collection).
    3.  The Detail Page (Deep dive into one item).
* **The Grid Connection:** At this stage, they are transitioning from standard block flow to Grid. They will use Grid to align the "specifications" of their items (e.g., a table-like layout for data without using actual `<table>` tags).
* **Minimalist Deliverable:** A functional 3-page site that demonstrates a consistent color palette, typography system, and clear navigation.

---

### Project 2: The "Editorial Feature" (Semester Finale)
**Focus:** CSS Grid Template Areas & Responsive Systems.
**The Concept:** An immersive, long-form digital magazine article (think *New York Times* "Snow Fall" or a *Pitchfork* feature). Students pick a complex topic (e.g., "The History of Artificial Intelligence" or "The Evolution of Skateboarding").

* **Why Grid Template Areas?** This technique is perfect for editorial design. Students can define a complex layout with "Asides," "Hero Images," "Pull Quotes," and "Body Text" and completely rearrange them for mobile using only the `grid-template-areas` property in a media query.



* **The In-Depth Requirement:** 1.  **Responsiveness:** The layout must fundamentally change at two breakpoints (Tablet and Mobile).
    2.  **Typography:** Implementation of a "Type Scale" (Heading 1, 2, 3, and Body) that stays consistent throughout.
    3.  **Complex Grid:** Use of `grid-column: span` and named areas to create a non-linear layout that doesn't look like a standard "Bootstrap" site.
* **Final Assessment:** A "Design Defense" where the student must explain why they chose a specific Grid area layout for the mobile experience vs. the desktop experience.

### Why this works for a 90-minute block:
With 90 minutes, you can implement a **"20/50/20" rule**:
1.  **20 Minutes (Lecture/Demo):** Teach a specific Grid property (e.g., `grid-area`).
2.  **50 Minutes (Studio):** Students apply that property directly to their "Editorial Feature." You move around the room for 1-on-1 "Desk Crits."
3.  **20 Minutes (Showcase/Debug):** Two students volunteer to show their layout on the big screen and discuss a bug they solved.
